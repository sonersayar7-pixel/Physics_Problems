## 6. Energy Dissipation: Step-by-Step

Based on the equations provided, we can identify the following starting conditions:
* **Initial height ($h_0$):** **2.0 m** (extracted from the final calculation)
* **Energy retained per bounce:** **70%** (represented as the coefficient 0.7)

---

### Step 1: Define the initial energy ($E_0$)
Before the object is dropped, its total initial energy is entirely gravitational potential energy, defined by the formula:

$$E_0 = mgh_0$$

---

### Step 2: Track energy through the bounces
The problem states that the object loses 30% of its energy upon impact, meaning it retains 70% of its energy after each bounce.

After the **first bounce**, the remaining energy ($E_1$) is:
$$E_1 = 0.7E_0$$

After the **second bounce**, the new energy ($E_2$) is 70% of the energy from the first bounce:
$$E_2 = 0.7E_1$$

Substitute $E_1$ into this equation to find the energy relative to the start:
$$E_2 = 0.7(0.7E_0)$$
$$E_2 = (0.7)^2 E_0$$
$$E_2 = 0.49E_0$$

This tells us that after two bounces, the object has 49% of its original energy.

---

### Step 3: Relate energy back to height
Because mass ($m$) and gravity ($g$) remain constant, gravitational potential energy is directly proportional to height ($E \propto h$). Therefore, the coefficient that applies to the energy also applies exactly to the height.

The height after the second bounce ($h_2$) will be 49% of the initial height ($h_0$):

$$h_2 = 0.49h_0$$

---

### Step 4: Calculate the final height
Substitute the initial height of 2.0 m into our final formula:

$$h_2 = 0.49 \cdot 2.0$$
$$h_2 = 0.98 \text{ m}$$

**Final Answer:** After two bounces, the object will rebound to a height of **0.98 m**.
