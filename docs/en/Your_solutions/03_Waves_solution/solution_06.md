## 6. The Wave Equation: Extracting Physical Properties

When analyzing a traveling wave, we often use a mathematical model. By comparing a specific wave's equation to the general wave formula, we can extract all of its physical properties piece by piece.

### Step 1: Comparing the Equations
The standard equation for a wave traveling in the positive x-direction is:
$$y(x,t) = A \sin(kx - \omega t)$$

The specific wave equation we are analyzing is:
$$y(x,t) = 0.05 \sin(2\pi x - 50\pi t)$$

By aligning these two equations, we can map the values from our specific wave to the variables in the general formula.

---

### Step 2: Extracting the Variables

**a) Amplitude ($A$)**
The amplitude represents the maximum height (or displacement) of the wave. By looking at the coefficient in front of the sine function, we can directly find the amplitude:
$$A = 0.05 \text{ m}$$

**b) Wavelength ($\lambda$)**
The wave number ($k$) is the coefficient of $x$. In our equation, $k = 2\pi$. Since the wave number is fundamentally defined as $\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$, we can set them equal to solve for the wavelength ($\lambda$):
$$k = 2\pi \implies \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} = 2\pi \implies \lambda = 1 \text{ m}$$

**c) Frequency ($f$)**
The angular frequency ($\omega$) is the coefficient of $t$. In our equation, $\omega = 50\pi$. Since angular frequency is defined as $2\pi f$, we can solve for the standard frequency ($f$):
$$\omega = 50\pi \implies 2\pi f = 50\pi \implies f = 25 \text{ Hz}$$

**d) Wave Speed ($v$)**
Now that we have both the frequency and the wavelength, we can calculate how fast the wave is traveling using the universal wave equation ($v = f\lambda$). 

*(Note: You can also calculate this directly from the original equation using the relationship $v = \frac{\omega}{k}$)*
$$v = f\lambda = 25 \text{ Hz} \times 1 \text{ m} = 25 \text{ m/s}$$

### Conclusion for Presentation
By mathematically dissecting the equation $y(x,t) = 0.05 \sin(2\pi x - 50\pi t)$, we can fully describe the physical wave. It is a wave that peaks at **0.05 meters** high, has a physical length of **1 meter**, vibrates **25 times per second**, and travels forward at a speed of **25 m/s**.
