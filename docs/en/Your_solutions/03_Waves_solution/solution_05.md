## 5. Echo Ranging: Calculating Distance with Sound

Echo ranging is a practical application of wave mechanics used in sonar, radar, and by animals like bats to determine how far away an object is. By measuring how long it takes for a sound wave to bounce off a surface (like a cliff) and return, we can calculate the exact distance.



### Step 1: The Distance-Time Relationship
When you shout at a cliff and hear an echo, the sound wave has actually traveled the distance between you and the cliff **twice**: once to reach the cliff, and once to bounce back to your ears. 

If the one-way distance to the cliff is $d$, the total distance the sound travels is $2d$. 

Using the standard physics formula for distance (Distance = Speed $\times$ Time), we can express this as:
$$2d = v \cdot t$$

To solve for the actual distance to the object ($d$), we simply divide the total distance by two:
$$d = \frac{v \cdot t}{2}$$

---

### Step 2: Identify the Known Variables
Let's assume we are in standard room-temperature air, and it takes exactly 1 second to hear the echo after making a sound.
* **Speed of sound ($v$):** $343 \text{ m/s}$
* **Total time for the echo ($t$):** $1 \text{ s}$

---

### Step 3: Calculate the Distance
Substitute our known variables into the rearranged formula:
$$d = \frac{343 \text{ m/s} \times 1 \text{ s}}{2}$$

First, calculate the total distance traveled:
$$d = \frac{343 \text{ m}}{2}$$

Then, divide by two for the one-way distance:
$$d = 171.5 \text{ m}$$

### Conclusion for Presentation
Because the sound took exactly 1 second to make the round trip, we know the sound wave covered a total distance of **343 meters**. By halving that result, we prove that the cliff reflecting the sound is exactly **171.5 meters** away.
