## Understanding Wave Properties: Sound in Different Mediums

When presenting on wave mechanics, one of the most important concepts to demonstrate is how a wave's physical environment changes its properties. In this breakdown, we will examine a standard **440 Hz** sound wave (the musical note A4) and calculate its wavelength as it travels through both air and water.

### 1. The Fundamental Equation
The foundation of our calculation is the universal wave equation, which defines the relationship between the speed of the wave, its frequency, and its wavelength. 

The formula is:
$$v = f\lambda$$

**Where:**
* **$v$ (Velocity/Speed):** How fast the wave travels through a given medium (measured in meters per second, $\text{m/s}$).
* **$f$ (Frequency):** The number of wave cycles that pass a fixed point per second (measured in Hertz, $\text{Hz}$). *Note: Frequency remains constant when a wave changes mediums.*
* **$\lambda$ (Wavelength):** The physical distance between two consecutive peaks of the wave (measured in meters, $\text{m}$).

To solve for wavelength ($\lambda$), we rearrange the formula:
$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

---

### 2. Case Study A: Sound Traveling Through Air
First, let's look at how our 440 Hz sound wave behaves in standard, room-temperature air.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**
* Speed of sound in air ($v_{air}$): $\approx 343 \text{ m/s}$
* Frequency ($f$): $440 \text{ Hz}$

**Step 2: Apply the Formula**
Substitute our known variables into the rearranged wave equation:
$$\lambda_{air} = \frac{v_{air}}{f}$$

**Step 3: Calculate the Result**
$$\lambda_{air} = \frac{343 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} \approx 0.78 \text{ m}$$

**Takeaway:** In the air, the physical distance between the peaks of a 440 Hz sound wave is roughly 78 centimeters.

---

### 3. Case Study B: Sound Traveling Through Water
Now, let's observe what happens when that exact same sound wave travels through water. Because water is denser and far less compressible than air, sound travels through it much faster.

**Step 1: Identify the Known Variables**
* Speed of sound in water ($v_{water}$): $\approx 1482 \text{ m/s}$
* Frequency ($f$): $440 \text{ Hz}$ (Remains unchanged)

**Step 2: Apply the Formula**
$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{v_{water}}{f}$$

**Step 3: Calculate the Result**
$$\lambda_{water} = \frac{1482 \text{ m/s}}{440 \text{ Hz}} \approx 3.37 \text{ m}$$

---

### 4. Conclusion for Presentation
By comparing the two results, we can draw a clear conclusion for the audience: 

Because the **frequency** ($440 \text{ Hz}$) is locked by the source of the sound, the **wavelength** must stretch to accommodate the increased **speed**. Therefore, when sound enters a medium where it travels faster (like transitioning from air to water), its wavelength drastically increases—in this case, expanding from $0.78 \text{ m}$ to $3.37 \text{ m}$.
