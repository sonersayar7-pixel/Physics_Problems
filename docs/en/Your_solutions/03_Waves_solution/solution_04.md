## 4. Phase Difference and Path Difference

When comparing two waves (or two points on the same wave), they are often not perfectly in sync. The **path difference** ($\Delta x$) measures the physical distance they are separated by, while the **phase difference** ($\Delta \phi$) measures this separation as an angle within a wave cycle.

### Step 1: The Relationship Formula
To convert a physical path difference into an angular phase difference, we use the following relationship:
$$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \Delta x$$

**Where:**
* **$\Delta \phi$:** Phase difference (measured in radians).
* **$\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$:** The wave number ($k$), which represents the number of radians per unit of distance. A full wave cycle is always $2\pi$ radians.
* **$\Delta x$:** Path difference (measured in the same units as the wavelength).

---

### Step 2: The Scenario
Suppose two waves travel slightly different distances to reach the exact same point, resulting in a path difference equal to one-third of a full wavelength.
* Path difference ($\Delta x$): $\frac{\lambda}{3}$

---

### Step 3: Calculating the Phase Difference
To find out how out-of-sync these waves are in terms of angles, substitute our known path difference into the formula:
$$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \left( \frac{\lambda}{3} \right)$$

The wavelengths ($\lambda$) in the numerator and denominator cancel each other out:
$$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{3} \text{ radians}$$

To convert this to degrees, we know that $2\pi$ radians equals **360°**, so one-third of that is **120°**.

### Conclusion for Presentation
A physical path difference of exactly $\frac{\lambda}{3}$ results in an angular phase difference of **$\frac{2\pi}{3}$ radians** (or **120°**). Physically, this means the two waves arrive exactly one-third of a cycle out of sync with each other.
