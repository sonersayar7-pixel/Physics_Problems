# Section 5: Electromagnetism II

## 1. Gauss's Law

**The Problem:** A point charge of **+2 C** is located at the origin. Calculate the electric flux through a spherical surface of radius **1 m** centered at the origin.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
Gauss's Law tells us that the total electric flux through a closed surface depends *only* on the total enclosed charge, not on the shape or size of the surface. 

The formula is:
$$\Phi_E = \frac{q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

* $q_{\text{enc}}$ is the enclosed charge (**2 C**).
* $\varepsilon_0$ (the vacuum permittivity) is a constant, approximately **8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m**.
* Notice that the radius (**1 m**) is a distractor here—it doesn't change the total flux!

$$\Phi_E = \frac{2}{8.854 \times 10^{-12}}$$

**Answer:** The electric flux is approximately **2.26 × 10¹¹ N·m²/C**.

## 2. Ampere's Law

**The Problem:** Two long, parallel wires are **10 cm** apart and carry currents of **5 A** in opposite directions. Calculate the magnitude and direction of the magnetic field at a point midway between the wires.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
First, let's find the magnetic field generated by just one wire at the midpoint. The midpoint is **5 cm** (**0.05 m**) away from each wire. The formula for the magnetic field of a long straight wire is:

$$B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2 \pi r}$$

Because the currents run in *opposite* directions, you can use the right-hand rule to see that their magnetic fields point in the *same* direction at the exact midpoint between them. Since they point the same way, we can simply add their magnitudes together.

$$B_{\text{total}} = B_1 + B_2 = 2 \left( \frac{\mu_0 I}{2 \pi r} \right) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{\pi r}$$

Let's plug in the values ($I =$ **5 A**, $r =$ **0.05 m**, and the magnetic permeability $\mu_0 =$ **4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A**):

$$B_{\text{total}} = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(5)}{\pi (0.05)} = \frac{20 \times 10^{-7}}{0.05}$$

**Answer:** The magnitude of the magnetic field is **4 × 10⁻⁵ T**. The direction is perpendicular to the plane containing the wires.

## 3. Biot-Savart Law

**The Problem:** A small segment of a line wire of length **0.1 m** carries a current of **3 A**. The segment is located **0.2 m** from point $P$ (perpendicular to the line connecting it). Calculate the magnetic field at $P$.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
The Biot-Savart law for a small segment of wire is:

$$dB = \frac{\mu_0 I \, dl \sin\theta}{4 \pi r^2}$$

Since the problem states the segment is perpendicular to the line connecting it to point $P$, the angle $\theta$ is **90°**, meaning $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$. 

Let's plug in our variables ($I =$ **3 A**, $dl =$ **0.1 m**, $r =$ **0.2 m**):

$$dB = \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}) (3) (0.1) (1)}{4 \pi (0.2)^2}$$
$$dB = \frac{10^{-7} \times 0.3}{0.04}$$

**Answer:** The magnetic field from this segment is **7.5 × 10⁻⁷ T**.

## 4. Magnetic Torque

**The Problem:** A rectangular loop (**10 cm** by **5 cm**) carries **2 A**. A magnetic field of **0.3 T** is applied parallel to the plane of the loop. Find the magnetic torque.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
First, we find the area $A$ of the loop: **0.1 m** × **0.05 m** = **0.005 m²**.
The formula for magnetic torque on a loop is:

$$\tau = N I A B \sin\theta$$

Here, $N = 1$ (just one loop). A crucial detail is $\theta$, which is the angle between the magnetic field and the *normal* (perpendicular) vector of the loop's area. Because the field is parallel to the loop's surface, it is perpendicular to the loop's normal vector. Therefore, $\theta =$ **90°**, and $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.

$$\tau = 1 \times 2 \times 0.005 \times 0.3 \times 1$$

**Answer:** The magnitude of the magnetic torque is **0.003 N·m**.

## 5. Energy Stored in a Capacitor

**The Problem:** A parallel-plate capacitor has area $S =$ **0.02 m²**, distance $d =$ **5 mm**, and voltage $U =$ **500 V**. Find Capacitance, Energy, Electric Field, and Force of attraction.

**Step-by-Step Explanations:**

**1. Capacitance ($C$):**
$$C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 S}{d}$$
$$C = \frac{(8.854 \times 10^{-12}) (0.02)}{0.005}$$
**Answer:** $C \approx$ **35.4 × 10⁻¹² F** (or **35.4 pF**).

**2. Energy ($U$):**
$$U_{\text{energy}} = \frac{1}{2} C V^2$$
$$U_{\text{energy}} = 0.5 \times (35.4 \times 10^{-12}) \times (500)^2$$
**Answer:** $U_{\text{energy}} \approx$ **4.43 × 10⁻⁶ J** (or **4.43 µJ**).

**3. Electric field intensity ($E$):**
$$E = \frac{V}{d}$$
$$E = \frac{500}{0.005}$$
**Answer:** $E =$ **100,000 V/m**.

**4. Force of attraction ($F$):**
The force between the plates can be calculated using the energy density or the field from a single plate:
$$F = \frac{1}{2} \varepsilon_0 E^2 S$$
$$F = 0.5 \times (8.854 \times 10^{-12}) \times (100,000)^2 \times 0.02$$
**Answer:** $F \approx$ **8.85 × 10⁻⁴ N** (or **0.885 mN**).

## 6. EM Wave Analysis

**The Problem:** You're given the electric field: $$E_y(x,t) = 100 \sin(10^7 x - \omega t)$$ in **V/m**. Find the direction, wavelength $\lambda$, angular frequency $\omega$, and the magnetic field equation.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
The standard wave equation form is $E(x,t) = E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t)$. 

* **Direction:** The $(kx - \omega t)$ phase term tells us the wave is traveling in the **+x direction**.
* **Wavelength ($\lambda$):** The wave number $k$ is **10⁷ rad/m**. 
    $$\lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} = \frac{2\pi}{10^7}$$
    **Answer:** $\lambda \approx$ **6.28 × 10⁻⁷ m** (or **628 nm**).
* **Angular Frequency ($\omega$):** In a vacuum, waves travel at the speed of light ($c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s). 
    $$\omega = c k = (3 \times 10^8)(10^7)$$
    **Answer:** $\omega =$ **3 × 10¹⁵ rad/s**.
* **Magnetic Field ($B$):** The amplitude of $B$ is linked to $E$ by $B_0 = E_0 / c$.
    $$B_0 = \frac{100}{3 \times 10^8} \approx 3.33 \times 10^{-7} \text{ T}$$
    Since the wave travels in $+x$ and $E$ is in $+y$, $B$ *must* point in the $+z$ direction so that the cross product $\vec{E} \times \vec{B}$ points in the direction of propagation.
    **Answer:** $$B_z(x,t) = 3.33 \times 10^{-7} \sin(10^7 x - 3 \times 10^{15} t) \text{ T}$$

## 7. Wavelength and Frequency

**The Problem:** Light with $\lambda =$ **550 nm**. What color is it, and what is its frequency?

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
**550 nm** (**5.5 × 10⁻⁷ m**) sits right in the middle of the visible spectrum.
* **Color:** This corresponds to a **yellow-green** color.
* **Frequency ($f$):** We use the wave speed equation $c = \lambda f$.
    $$f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{5.5 \times 10^{-7}}$$
    **Answer:** $f \approx$ **5.45 × 10¹⁴ Hz** (or **545 THz**).

## 8. EM Spectrum

**The Problem:** Order the following by *increasing* wavelength: Infrared, Ultraviolet, Microwaves, X-rays, Radio waves, Gamma rays.

**Answer:**
Shortest wavelengths have the highest energy, while longest wavelengths have the lowest energy. In order of shortest to longest (increasing):
1.  **Gamma rays**
2.  **X-rays**
3.  **Ultraviolet**
4.  **Infrared**
5.  **Microwaves**
6.  **Radio waves**

## 9. Refraction (Snell's Law)

**The Problem:** Light travels from air ($n_1 = 1.00$) to glass ($n_2 = 1.50$) at an incidence angle of **30°**. Find the angle of refraction.

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
We apply Snell's Law, which relates the angles and indices of refraction:
$$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$
$$1.00 \times \sin(30^\circ) = 1.50 \times \sin\theta_2$$
Since $\sin(30^\circ) = 0.5$:
$$0.5 = 1.50 \sin\theta_2$$
$$\sin\theta_2 = \frac{0.5}{1.5} = \frac{1}{3}$$
To find the angle, take the inverse sine ($\arcsin$) of **1/3**.

**Answer:** $\theta_2 \approx$ **19.5°**.

## 10. Speed of Light in Media

**The Problem:** What is the speed of light in a diamond ($n =$ **2.42**)?

**Step-by-Step Explanation:**
The index of refraction ($n$) tells you how much slower light travels in a material compared to a vacuum.
$$v = \frac{c}{n}$$
$$v = \frac{3 \times 10^8}{2.42}$$

**Answer:** The speed of light in the diamond is approximately **1.24 × 10⁸ m/s**.
